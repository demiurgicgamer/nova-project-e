## Topic Summary

**What the child learns:**
- What a fraction is (part of a whole, equal parts)
- Numerator and denominator — what each represents
- Comparing fractions with the same denominator
- Adding and subtracting fractions with the same denominator
- Reading fractions in real-world contexts

**Prerequisites:** Basic division concept, understanding of equal sharing
**Leads to:** Equivalent fractions, mixed numbers, fractions with different denominators (separate arcs)

## Stage 2 — Concept

### Core explanation (EN)

Nova uses Hook Story A (pizza) as the vehicle.

**Step 1 — The whole:**
> "That pizza — 8 equal slices — is our whole. The whole is always cut into equal parts. That's important: equal parts. A pizza cut into uneven slices doesn't count."

**Step 2 — The denominator:**
> "The number at the bottom of a fraction tells us how many equal parts the whole is cut into. 8 slices total → 8 goes on the bottom. We call it the denominator — the bottom is the denominator."

**Step 3 — The numerator:**
> "The number at the top tells us how many parts we have. You took 3 slices → 3 goes on top. We call it the numerator — the top is our number."

**Step 4 — Reading it:**
> "So you ate 3 out of 8 slices. Written as a fraction: 3/8. We say 'three eighths.'"

**Whiteboard:**
```
    3     ← numerator   (your slices)
   ───
    8     ← denominator (total equal slices)

  "three eighths of the pizza"
```

**Stage 2 comprehension check (not graded — just confirmation):**
> "So your friend took 2 slices from the same pizza. Without me helping — what fraction did they eat? Just tell me the two numbers."

Expected: 2/8 (two eighths).
If correct → move to Stage 3.
If wrong → reteach with Angle B (chocolate bar) before retrying check.

---

### Reteach Angle B — chocolate bar (EN)
> "Let's forget the pizza for a second. Imagine a chocolate bar broken into 6 equal pieces. You eat 2 pieces. The total pieces is our denominator — 6. The pieces you ate is our numerator — 2. So you ate 2/6 of the chocolate bar. Now back to the pizza — same idea, different numbers."

### Reteach Angle C — time (EN)
> "Think of an hour — 60 minutes, but let's say it's split into 4 equal chunks of 15 minutes each. You spend 1 chunk watching TV. Total chunks: 4. Your chunks: 1. Fraction: 1/4 of the hour. Same idea everywhere."

---

### Core explanation (FR-CA)

**Step 1:**
> "La poutine — coupée en 8 portions égales — c'est notre tout. Le tout est toujours divisé en parties égales."

**Step 2:**
> "Le nombre en bas de la fraction nous dit en combien de parties égales le tout est divisé. 8 portions → 8 en bas. On appelle ça le dénominateur — le bas, c'est le dénominateur."

**Step 3:**
> "Le nombre en haut nous dit combien de parties on a. Tu as pris 3 portions → 3 en haut. On appelle ça le numérateur — en haut, c'est notre nombre."

**Step 4:**
> "Donc tu as mangé 3 portions sur 8. En fraction : 3/8. On dit 'trois huitièmes.'"

**Vérification Stage 2 (FR):**
> "Ton ami a pris 2 portions de la même poutine. Sans mon aide — quelle fraction a-t-il mangée? Donne-moi juste les deux chiffres."

## Stage 3 — Guided Problem

### EN

**Problem:**
> "A chocolate bar has 6 equal pieces. Maya eats 2 pieces. What fraction of the chocolate bar did Maya eat?"

**Nova's step-by-step:**
1. "How many pieces is the whole bar cut into?" → 6 → "That's our denominator — bottom number."
2. "How many pieces did Maya eat?" → 2 → "That's our numerator — top number."
3. "So what fraction did Maya eat?" → 2/6

**MC choices:**
- ✅ 2/6
- ❌ 6/2 (flipped — common mistake)
- ❌ 2/4 (wrong total)
- ❌ 4/6 (remaining pieces, not eaten pieces)

**Intervention hints:**
- Level 1: "Let's use smaller numbers — if a bar had 3 pieces and Maya ate 1, the fraction would be 1/3. Now back to 6 pieces and 2 eaten."
- Level 2: "Remember our pizza? 8 slices total, you took 3 — that was 3/8. This chocolate bar works exactly the same way. 6 pieces total, Maya took 2..."
- Level 3: "Just the bottom number first — how many pieces total?" → wait → "Good. Now just the top — how many did Maya eat?" → wait → "Put them together with a line between."

**Common misconception:** Child writes 6/2 — they know both numbers but put them in the wrong order. Nova response: "You've got the right numbers — just check which one goes on top. Which number is the part Maya ate, and which is the total?"

### FR-CA

**Choix:**

- ❌ 6/2
- ❌ 2/4

## Stage 4 — Practice

### Problem 4a — Medium (EN)

**Problem:**
> "A water bottle has 8 equal sips worth of water. Leon drinks 5 sips. What fraction of the bottle did Leon drink?"

**Answer:** 5/8
**MC choices:**
- ✅ 5/8
- ❌ 3/8 (remaining water, not drunk)
- ❌ 5/3 (flipped with wrong denominator)
- ❌ 8/5 (flipped)

**Intervention hints:**
- Level 1: "Just focus on two things — total sips in the bottle, and how many Leon drank."
- Level 2: "Pizza again — 8 slices, you took 5. What fraction? Same numbers, same idea."
- Level 3: "Bottom number first — how many sips total?"

**Common misconception:** Child writes 3/8 — they calculated the remaining water. Nova: "3/8 is how much is LEFT in the bottle. The question asks how much Leon DRANK — which number is that?"

---

### Problem 4a — Medium (FR-CA)

**Problème:**
> "Une bouteille d'eau contient 8 gorgées égales. Léon en boit 5. Quelle fraction de la bouteille Léon a-t-il bue?"

**Réponse:** 5/8

### Problem 4b — Medium-Hard (EN)

**Problem:**
> "Sara ate 3/8 of a pizza. Tom ate 4/8 of the same pizza. Who ate more, and how do you know?"

**Answer:** Tom ate more. Same denominator (8), so compare numerators: 4 > 3.

**MC choices:**
- ✅ Tom — because 4/8 is more than 3/8
- ❌ Sara — because she ate first
- ❌ They ate the same — the denominator is the same
- ❌ Can't tell — we don't know the pizza size

**Key teaching point:** When denominators are the same, just compare numerators. The bottom number tells you the size of each piece — same size pieces means more pieces = more pizza.

**Intervention hints:**
- Level 1: "Forget fractions — if I gave you 3 slices and Tom 4 slices from the same pizza, who has more?"
- Level 2: "The bottom number — 8 — is the same for both. That means each slice is the same size. So who has more slices?"
- Level 3: "How many slices does Sara have? How many does Tom have? Which number is bigger?"

---

### Problem 4b — FR-CA

**Problème:**
> "Sara a mangé 3/8 d'une pizza. Tom a mangé 4/8 de la même pizza. Qui en a mangé le plus? Comment le sais-tu?"

**Réponse:** Tom — parce que 4/8 est plus grand que 3/8 (même dénominateur, on compare les numérateurs).

---

### Problem 4c — Hard (EN)

**Problem:**
> "A garden is divided into 10 equal sections. Rosa planted flowers in 3 sections. Her brother planted in 4 sections. What fraction of the garden is planted altogether?"

**Answer:** 7/10
**Method:** 3/10 + 4/10 = 7/10 (same denominator — add numerators, keep denominator)

**MC choices:**
- ✅ 7/10
- ❌ 7/20 (added both numerators AND denominators — very common mistake)
- ❌ 12/10 (multiplied instead of added)
- ❌ 3/4 (ignored denominators entirely)

**Key teaching point:** When denominators are the same, add only the numerators. The denominator stays the same — the total number of sections in the garden doesn't change just because you planted more.

**Intervention hints:**
- Level 1: "If Rosa has 3 apples and her brother has 4 apples, how many apples together? Now write that over 10."
- Level 2: "Back to the pizza — 8 slices. You took 3, friend took 4. Together you took 3+4=7 slices. Written as fraction: 7/8. Garden works the same — 10 sections total, 3+4 sections planted = ?"
- Level 3: "Just the top numbers — 3 sections plus 4 sections equals how many sections? Now what's the total number of sections in the garden?"

**Common misconception:** Child writes 7/20. Nova: "I see where that came from — you added the bottom numbers too. But the garden still has 10 sections total. The total doesn't change when you plant more. So what stays on the bottom?"

---

### Problem 4c — FR-CA

**Problème:**
> "Un jardin est divisé en 10 sections égales. Rosa a planté des fleurs dans 3 sections. Son frère en a planté dans 4 sections. Quelle fraction du jardin est plantée en tout?"

**Réponse:** 7/10

## Stage 5 — Capstone

### EN

**Problem:**
> "A recipe needs 3/8 cup of sugar.
> Mia has already added 1/8 cup.
> How much more sugar does she still need to add?
> And what fraction of the total sugar has she added so far?"

**Two parts:**
1. 3/8 − 1/8 = 2/8 (still needs 2/8 cup more)
2. 1/8 of the total has been added so far

**No scaffolding. Nova presents and waits.**

**Single guiding question (between attempt 1 and attempt 2 only):**
> "What's the total amount of sugar the recipe needs? And how much has Mia put in already? Start with those two pieces."

**If passed:**
> "You just solved a two-step fractions problem without any help. That took understanding what a fraction means, how to subtract fractions, and reading a real-world situation. That's real mathematics — and you did it."

**If failed after 2 attempts:**
> "This one has two steps and that's genuinely hard. Let's come back to it next time — you've already learned a lot today. We'll nail it together next session."
> → Return to Stage 4 next session with one new problem, then retry Stage 5 with a different capstone.

**Alternate capstone (for retry):**
> "A ribbon is 8/10 metres long. Kenji cuts off 3/10 metres. What fraction of the ribbon is left? And what fraction did he cut off?"

---

### FR-CA

**Problème:**
> "Une recette nécessite 3/8 de tasse de sucre.
> Mia a déjà ajouté 1/8 de tasse.
> Combien de sucre doit-elle encore ajouter?
> Et quelle fraction du sucre total a-t-elle ajouté jusqu'à présent?"

**Réponses:**
1. 3/8 − 1/8 = 2/8 (elle a encore besoin de 2/8 de tasse)
2. 1/8 du total a été ajouté

**Question guide (entre tentative 1 et 2 seulement):**
> "Quelle quantité totale de sucre la recette demande-t-elle? Et combien Mia a-t-elle déjà mis? Commence par ces deux éléments."

**Capstone alternatif (FR-CA):**
> "Un ruban mesure 8/10 de mètre. Kenji en coupe 3/10. Quelle fraction du ruban reste-t-il? Et quelle fraction a-t-il coupée?"

## Common Misconceptions — Quick Reference

| Misconception | Example | Nova's approach |
|---|---|---|
| Flip numerator/denominator | Writes 8/3 instead of 3/8 | "You have the right numbers — which is the total, which is your part?" |
| Add denominators | 3/8 + 4/8 = 7/16 | "The garden still has 10 sections. Does planting more change the total?" |
| Compare fractions by numerator only without checking denominator | Says 3/5 > 3/8 without reasoning | Covered in next arc (different denominators) |
| Use remaining parts instead of taken parts | Writes 5/8 instead of 3/8 | "The question asks how much she ATE — which number is that?" |

## Review Mode (post-mastery)

Once Stage 5 is passed, the child enters **Review mode** for this topic.
In future sessions, Nova surfaces one Stage 4–5 level problem per session for this topic.
No hook, no concept — just the problem, as a warm-up or closing challenge.
This prevents forgetting without repeating the full arc.
